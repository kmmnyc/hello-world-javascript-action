# Voltaire’s Bastards

In **Voltaire’s Bastards**, John Ralston Saul argues that the **Enlightenment's** ideal of reason has been corrupted into a rigid, amoral **dictatorship of management**. He contends that modern society is dominated by a **technocratic elite**—trained in systems and logic—who prioritize **methodology and efficiency** over humanism and ethics. By tracing the evolution of power from **Cardinal Richelieu** to contemporary figures like **Robert McNamara**, Saul illustrates how specialized expertise has fractured language and marginalized **democratic participation**. He suggests that our obsession with **rational certainty** has actually fueled global crises, including the arms trade and systemic economic failure. Ultimately, the book calls for a return to **humanist doubt** and individual consciousness to challenge the sterile dogma of the current **managerial age**.

### Voltaire’s Bastards: The Dictatorship of Reason in the West

#### Executive Summary

The modern Western world is currently governed by a "Dictatorship of Reason"—a narrow system that has swollen into an amoral ideology. While the 18th-century philosophers, led by Voltaire, embraced reason as a tool for humanism and social reform, their descendants have inverted the equation. In the contemporary era, reason has become a disinterested administrative method, divorced from ethical structures and common sense.

The source context argues that Western society is trapped in a "tailspin" characterized by a passive, cynical elite, a hypnotic obsession with methodology over purpose, and the fragmentation of knowledge into "feudal fiefdoms" of expertise. This system has replaced leadership with management and democracy with a "theology of pure power" composed of organization, technology, and information. The only path toward a conscious, healthy society is through the recovery of humanism and the "virtue of doubt"—the ability to live with uncertainty as a creative force.

#### I. The Ideology of Reason

##### The Inversion of the Socratic Equation

The document highlights a fundamental shift in the application of knowledge:

- **The Socratic Conviction:** Virtues are forms of knowledge; therefore, no man willingly does wrong.
- **The Modern Rational Inversion:** Modern elites justify doing wrong precisely *because* they know. Rational sophistication has led to a profound cynicism where expertise is used to justify inaction regarding poverty, debt, and environmental crises.

##### Reason as Dogma

Reason is no longer a tool for inquiry but a "dogma, devoid of direction and disguised as disinterested inquiry." It presents itself as the solution to the very problems it creates. This system favors those whose talents lie in manipulation and who possess a taste for power in its purest forms.

### II. The Modern Elite: Technocrats and Heroes

The rational power structure is characterized by two complementary but distinct types of leadership: the Technocrat and the Hero.

#### 1. The Technocrat

The technocrat is the "essential middleman" between the people and the divinity of power.

- **Characteristics:** Detached, manipulative, and obsessed with detail. They are "grease jockeys" trained to make the engine of government and business run, but they have no idea where to steer the vehicle.
- **Training:** Places like Harvard Business School and the French *École Nationale d’Administration* (ENA) focus on the "science of systems management." They develop methods for recognizing solutions that satisfy the system rather than solving actual problems.
- **Amorality:** The technocrat is "unpremeditated" when doing good or evil. They understand events only within the internal logic of the system.

#### 2. The Hero (and the Dictator)

The Hero emerges when the public becomes impatient with the boredom or inefficiency of technocratic management.

- **The Napoleonic Model:** Napoleon Bonaparte serves as the definitive model of the rational Hero—a "magician" who replaces memory and history with himself.
- **The Mechanism of Bondage:** The Hero uses a "mystical" and "tragic" persona to bond with a disturbed population. This charisma, combined with rational methods, creates the recipe for the modern absolute dictator.
- **The Reality of the Hero:** While presented as idealistic, the Hero often leads to political, military, and financial excess (e.g., Napoleon's destruction of the first modern republics).

### IV. Systematic Failures of the Rational Age

#### The "Miracle of the Loaves" and the Debt Crisis

The source context posits that modern economic leadership is in a "downward spiral":

- **The African Failure:** Rational debt theories were used to destroy African economies.
- **Internal Western Debt:** These same failed theories are now being applied to Western democracies, undermining public programs and expanding the rich-poor divide without any admission of error.
- **Alzheimer's Quality:** A civilization that promotes form over content suffers from evaporating memory, failing to learn from previous failures.

#### The International Arms Trade

The arms trade is cited as the "cleanest and neatest" example of amoral rationalism. While senior civil servants and corporate leaders are responsible for 90% of the trade, they are viewed as "dispassionate" while the trade itself fuels global instability.

#### The Cult of the "Star"

As technocrats (who are of little general interest) took over the levers of power, they were replaced in the public imagination by "celebrities" or "stars." These figures provide a distraction similar to the courts of the 18th century, occupying the press and public dreams while having no relationship to real power.

### V. The Distortion of Knowledge and Language

#### Specialization and Silence

Knowledge has been divided into "feudal fiefdoms of expertise," making general understanding impossible and distrusted.

- **Private Language:** The "wordsmiths" of power use complexity and obscurity to subject language to a "private" realm, effectively castrating the public imagination.
- **The Individual in a Box:** Individuals are locked within their specializations, afraid to speak out on important questions for fear of risking their careers or stepping outside their "area of expertise."

#### The Dictatorship of Vocabulary

Establishment power has co-opted vocabulary to determine answers by the way questions are posed. Words like "fairness," "efficiency," and "rationalization" are used as icons to replace thought and justify actions such as closing factories or cutting social benefits.

### VI. Humanism: The Minority School

The source context identifies Humanism as the "minority school" and the true glory of the West. It is represented by figures like Socrates, Erasmus, Pascal Paoli, and Thomas Jefferson.

- **Characteristics of Humanism:** Based on responsible individualism, engaged citizenry, empathy, and the use of doubt.
- **The Conflict:** Humanism is the "profound enemy" of the dominant rational approach. Rationality seeks absolute answers, while Humanism seeks a more complex, "reasonable" approach to living.
- **Exclusion of Women:** The Age of Reason is described as a "male reality." Women were historically excluded from the creation of these structures and were often viewed as symbols of the "irrational."

### VII. Conclusion: The Virtue of Doubt

The briefing document concludes that Western civilization is in a state of "cultural suicide" by abandoning empathy and consciousness in favor of efficiency. The only way to regain control is to:

1. **Embrace Doubt:** Recognize uncertainty as a sign of strength and a creative force.
2. **Reject Absolute Logic:** Break with the "superstition of knowledge" and the "divine revelations of reason."
3. **Prioritize Humanism:** Re-establish a sensible relationship between ideas and action, focusing on the "public weal" as the dominant passion.

As the source context notes: *"Reason is no more than structure... Darkness seems scarcely different from light, with the web of structure and logic woven thick across both."* Recovery requires cutting away these layers to restore common sense and morality.
